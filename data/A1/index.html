1. Alphabets

The French alphabet has 26 letters, just like the English alphabet. However, the pronunciation of some letters is different. Here is a list of the French alphabet with their pronunciations:

  1. A - ah
  2. B - bay
  3. C - say
  4. D - day
  5. E - uh
  6. F - eff
  7. G - zhay
  8. H - ash
  9. I - ee
  10. J - zhee
  11. K - kah
  12. L - ell
  13. M - emm
  14. N - enn
  15. O - oh
  16. P - pay
  17. Q - koo
  18. R - air
  19. S - ess
  20. T - tay
  21. U - oo
  22. V - vay
  23. W - doo-blay-vay
  24. X - eeks
  25. Y - ee-grek
  26. Z - zed

My thoughts

- When I was learning french, I found out alphabets E, G, J, Q, R, U has different pronunciations than English. So you need to learn carefully

- Alphabet "R" can be tricky for English speakers, as it is pronounced from the back of the throat. It takes practice to get it right.

- The way we pronunce alphabets W, X, Y in french are not going to be the same we pronounce in the french sentences (More on that later part of the A1)

- In french we don't pronunce "H" it always remains silent, so you will never hear "H" in a french sentence.

- We speak till the ending letter before vowels, example "prende" is pronunced as "prend" and "prendre" is pronounced as "prendr". This is very important to understand, as it will help you in pronouncing the words correctly.

2. Consonant sounds

In French, consonants can have different sounds depending on their position in a word and the letters that follow them.

  • [e] - et, ez, er, é = pronunce it like "eay" (ए)
    eg. avez, allé, aller, paler, préféré


  • [i] - i, y, = pronunce it like "ee" (ई)
    eg. mini, anny


  • [E] - est, es, è, ê, ai = pronunce it like "aee" (ओ)
    eg. mais, lève, lèver, être


  • [a] - a, â, à = pronunce it like "aaa" (आ)
    eg. anna, anny, ananas


  • [ui] - u, ù, û, eu = prounce it like "uooo" (उ)
    eg. sur, sûr, ursûle


  • [ø] - eu, e = prounce it like "uo"
    eg. mercredi, deux, veux


  • [œ] - eg. œuf, neuf, seul = pronunce like "owa"


My thoughts

- C,F,L,R are the only consonants that are pronounced the same in both French and English.

- The est, es, et can be pronunced as "eay" (ए) but if they come together we have say 1 word in "eay" (ए) and "aee" (ओ) depending on the word.

- try to learn all uh sounds they are very important in french, as they are used in many words.

4. Articles

Articles in french are like the, a in English. They are used to indicate the noun that follows them. There are two types of articles in French: definite and indefinite.

  • Definite articles: le (masculine), la (feminine), l' (before a vowel or silent h), les (plural)
  • Indefinite articles: un (masculine), une (feminine), des (plural)

My thoughts

- The most difficult part of articles is when to use masculine and feminine articles. In French, nouns are either masculine or feminine, and this affects the article used. For example, "le" is used for masculine le livre nouns and "la" is used for la plage for feminine nouns.

5. Possessive Adjectives

Possessive adjectives in French are used to indicate ownership or possession. Like if i say "my book" in English, in French it would be "mon livre".



My thoughts

- The most difficult part of possessive adjectives is when to use masculine and feminine adjectives.

- for example, mon livre (my book) is masculine, so we use "mon" as the possessive adjective. But if we say ma voiture (my car), it is feminine, so we use "ma" as the possessive adjective.

6. Reflexive

Reflexive verbs in French are used to indicate that the subject of the verb is also the object of the verb. For example, "I wash myself" in French is "Je me lave".

  • Je me (I)
  • Tu te (You)
  • Il/Elle/On se (He/She/One)
  • Nous nous (We)
  • Vous vous (You - formal/plural)
  • Ils/Elles se (They - masculine/feminine)

My thoughts

- So you will be thinking what is the difference between reflexive and possessive adjectives? The difference is that reflexive verbs are used to indicate that the subject of the verb is also the object of the verb, while possessive adjectives are used to indicate ownership or possession.

- For example, "I wash myself" in French is "Je me lave", while "my book" in French is "mon livre".

- You will learn more when you will learn about the verbs in French, but for now just understand that reflexive verbs are used to indicate that the subject of the verb is also the object of the verb.

7. Numbers

Numbers in French are used to indicate quantity or order. They are similar to numbers in English, but there are some differences in pronunciation and spelling.

1. Numbers 1-16 (memorize these)

  • 1 - un
  • 2 - deux
  • 3 - trois
  • 4 - quatre
  • 5 - cinq
  • 6 - six
  • 7 - sept
  • 8 - huit
  • 9 - neuf
  • 10 - dix
  • 11 - onze
  • 12 - douze
  • 13 - treize
  • 14 - quatorze
  • 15 - quinze
  • 16 - seize

2. Numbers 17-19 (10 + unit)

  • 17 → dix-sept (10 + 7)
  • 18 → dix-huit (10 + 8)
  • 19 → dix-neuf (10 + 9)

3. Numbers 20-69 (tens + units)

  • 20 → vingt
  • 21 → vingt et un (20 and 1)
  • 22 → vingt-deux (20 + 2)
  • 30 → trente
  • 31 → trente et un (30 and 1)
  • 40 → quarante
  • 50 → cinquante
  • 60 → soixante

4. Numbers 70-79 (60 + 10-19)

  • 70 → soixante-dix (60 + 10)
  • 71 → soixante et onze (60 + 11)
  • 72 → soixante-douze (60 + 12)
  • …
  • 79 → soixante-dix-neuf (60 + 19)

5. Numbers 80-99 (4 X 20 + units)

  • 80 → quatre-vingts (4 X 20)
  • 81 → quatre-vingt-un (4 X 20 + 1)
  • 82 → quatre-vingt-deux (4 X 20 + 2)
  • 90 → quatre-vingt-dix (4 X 20 + 10)
  • 91 → quatre-vingt-onze (4 X 20 + 11)
  • 99 → quatre-vingt-dix-neuf (4 X 20 + 19)

6. Big Numbers

  • 100 → cent
  • 1,000 → mille
  • 1,000,000 → un million
  • 1,000,000,000 → un milliard

7. How French Numbers Work (Concept)

  • Additive: 57 → cinquante-sept → 50 + 7
  • Multiplicative: 80 → quatre-vingts → 4 × 20
  • Combination: 97 → quatre-vingt-dix-sept → 4 × 20 + 17

Tips:

  • Numbers ending in 1 often use et un.
  • Numbers above 60 often reuse smaller numbers creatively (e.g., 70 = 60+10, 90 = 4×20+10).
  • Once you see the pattern, you can build or read any number in French.

My thoughts

- The most difficult part of numbers is the pronunciation. The numbers 70, 80, and 90 are pronounced differently in French than in English. For example, 70 is pronounced "soixante-dix" (60+10), 80 is pronounced "quatre-vingts" (4*20), and 90 is pronounced "quatre-vingt-dix" (4*20+10).
